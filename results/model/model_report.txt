1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
This report details the architectural design and performance characteristics of the 
binary classification systems developed to predict loan defaults. To satisfy both 
predictive accuracy and regulatory explainability, two distinct modeling architectures 
were developed and contrasted: a Global Black-Box approach (LightGBM + SHAP) and 
an Econometric "Double-Tree" approach (Piecewise Linear Model).

2. DATA ARCHITECTURE & PREPROCESSING
Both models utilized a robust, stateful pipeline engineered from 7 interconnected 
financial tables. 
- Aggregation: 1:N and 1:N:M time-series data were flattened using a Two-Step 
  Aggregation strategy to prevent "data blurring" across multiple credit accounts.
- Preprocessing: A stateful `BaselinePreprocessor` was utilized. Weight of Evidence 
  (WOE) encoding was applied to stabilize linear models, and `RobustScaler` was 
  implemented to protect variance from extreme financial anomalies (e.g., $3.7M 
  prepayments) without manual Winsorizing.
- Feature Selection: Recursive Feature Elimination (RFE) via coordinate descent 
  (liblinear) successfully isolated the most predictive features, mitigating the 
  curse of dimensionality.

3. APPROACH 1: THE GLOBAL BLACK-BOX (LightGBM + SHAP)
- Algorithm: LightGBM (Gradient Boosted Decision Trees).
- Mechanics: A global ensemble of leaf-wise decision trees.
- Interpretability: Post-hoc explanation using SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP).
- Strengths: Achieves the highest baseline predictive power (ROC-AUC). It natively 
  handles non-linear feature interactions and monotonic scaling.
- Weaknesses: SHAP values are approximations of the model's behavior, not the 
  mathematical truth of the algorithm. Global trees fail to provide a direct, 
  auditable mathematical equation for why a specific client was rejected, posing 
  regulatory friction under strict financial compliance laws.

4. APPROACH 2: THE ECONOMETRIC DOUBLE-TREE (Piecewise Linear Model)
- Algorithm: Custom Piecewise Linear Model (Model Tree).
- Mechanics: 
  1. A shallow, heavily regularized Decision Tree acts as a global segmenter, 
     clustering the training data into distinct terminal nodes (Borrower Personas).
  2. Within each leaf, the data is isolated, and a distinct, independent Logistic 
     Regression model is trained specifically on that micro-segment.
- Interpretability: Inherently transparent. 
- Strengths: Bridging the "Accuracy vs. Interpretability" gap. The global tree 
  captures the complex, non-linear macro interactions that simple linear models 
  miss. However, because the terminal predictions are generated by standard Logistic 
  Regression equations, the model outputs exact, mathematically auditable coefficients 
  for every decision. 
- Findings: (See `leaf_coefficient_comparison.png`). The Double-Tree approach 
  revealed that risk drivers are heavily dependent on the customer segment. For 
  example, Credit Utilization may be a massive default driver for Persona A, but 
  statistically irrelevant for Persona B. A global linear model incorrectly averages 
  this effect out; the Piecewise model perfectly isolates it.

5. CONCLUSION & BUSINESS IMPACT
The LightGBM model remains the optimal choice for pure predictive accuracy. However, 
the newly implemented Piecewise Linear approach provides an elegant econometric 
solution for deployment in highly regulated environments. By segmenting the population 
before regressing, the business can audit the exact mathematical weight of every 
decision while maintaining competitive predictive power.
